Introduction: From Individual Logic to Coordination Across Differences

This essay introduces formal logical tools that support sophisticated reasoning across cultural differences—tools formalized in the FAIM-QIRF (Functional Agent Interaction Model - Quantified Information Representation Framework) through mathematical specifications and empirical protocols. While traditional logic focuses on individual reasoning, capability-based logic provides instruments for coordination across authentic differences at both individual and coalition levels.

The core insight—that formal logical tools become more powerful when they enable coalition formation across different Personal Reality Frameworks—emerges from analysis of international gaming sessions, modal logic development, and coordination studies. What traditional approaches see as irreconcilable cultural differences, capability-based reasoning suggests may function as complementary coordination strengths.

The FAIM-QIRF formalization operationalizes these insights through an integrated system combining Alternating-Time Temporal Logic (ATL), STIT logic, enhanced Coalition Logic, Cross-Scale Correspondence, and game-theoretic semantics. Whether you approach logic as a philosopher seeking theoretical precision, a practitioner needing decision-making tools, or a coordinator managing multicultural challenges, these formal methods provide worldview-agnostic instruments for reasoning effectiveness.

The Cultural Coordination Challenge

Traditional logic provides tools for individual reasoning but faces difficulties with coordination challenges in our interconnected world. How do people with fundamentally different belief systems, decision-making processes, and values work together effectively without sacrificing their authentic identities?

The Integration Approach: The answer lies in understanding how formal logical tools can enable coalition formation through functional equivalence rather than content agreement. Different cultural approaches to reasoning may achieve equivalent coordination outcomes while preserving their distinctive authenticity criteria.

The Core Framework: Capability-Based Propositions and Functional Equivalence

The foundation of capability-based logic rests on an insight validated through coordination research: instead of asking "Do we all believe the same thing?" we ask "Can we each execute strategies that achieve equivalent coordination outcomes?" This shifts logic from requiring consensus to enabling functional cooperation across authentic differences.

Functional Equivalence Definition: Two agents are functionally equivalent for a goal when they can each contribute to achieving that goal while maintaining their own authenticity criteria. A utilitarian calculating optimal outcomes and a deontologist following moral duties may coordinate effectively on environmental protection through functionally equivalent approaches.

ATL (Alternating-Time Temporal Logic): Strategic Coordination Across Cultures

ATL functions as a cross-cultural coordination analysis system, moving beyond predictions to strategic capability assessment that respects different cultural approaches to planning and execution.

Enhanced Mathematical Structure:

  • ⟨⟨A⟩⟩φ means "Coalition A has culturally-authentic joint strategies to ensure φ happens"
  • This represents capability claims that preserve each member's authenticity criteria

Cross-Cultural Application Example: Consider the challenge "Our international climate coalition can implement effective emission reductions." The ATL Coalition Logic approach asks: "Does our coalition have culturally-authentic strategies that each member can execute within their own framework to achieve functionally equivalent emission reductions?" This transforms cultural conflict into practical capability coordination.

Enhanced Coalition Logic: Applications for Cross-Cultural Coordination

FAIM-QIRF's enhanced Coalition Logic explores coordination across different Personal Reality Frameworks through functional equivalence. Different cultural backgrounds may contribute complementary coordination strengths:

  • WEIRD cultures: May excel at impersonal coordination and institutional scalability.
  • Traditional cultures: May excel at intensive cooperation and contextual reasoning.
  • Religious cultures: May provide transcendent coordination across cultural boundaries.
  • Indigenous cultures: May contribute long-term thinking and ecological integration.

STIT Logic (Seeing To It That): Individual Agency Within Coalition Contexts

STIT logic analyzes individual agency within coalition frameworks. [i stit: φ] means "Agent i has it within their power to ensure φ happens while maintaining their cultural authenticity and coalition relationships." This framework distinguishes between authentic contributions that align with an agent's cultural framework and forced assimilation where an agent must adopt a dominant culture's approach.

The Temporal Coherence Framework: Mathematical Reasoning Across Time and Culture

The Temporal Coherence Framework addresses time, change, and cultural differences simultaneously through the ATCF (Adaptive Temporal Coherence Function), which measures whether an agent can maintain consistent reasoning across past, present, and future while participating in cross-cultural coalitions.

Game-Theoretic Semantics: Truth as Winning Strategy Across Cultures

Game-theoretic semantics becomes particularly useful for cross-cultural reasoning by treating truth as "having a winning strategy in a cross-cultural logical game." You "win" if you can respond successfully to serious challenges from multiple cultural viewpoints, demonstrating that your claim holds up across different frameworks of validation.
